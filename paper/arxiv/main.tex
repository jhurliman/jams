\begin{abstract}
We present \jams{}, an open-source music-information-retrieval (MIR) service for
electronic dance music (EDM) covering key, tempo, beats, structural segmentation,
source separation, and per-stem MIDI transcription, in which every capability is backed
by a reproducible evaluation harness and every reported number regenerates from
committed code. We report four main findings. (1)~A calibration result that reframes
how key-detection results are compared: re-running the strongest published system (the
madmom key CNN, published 0.746 weighted) on our $n{=}567$ usable-track subset of
GiantSteps~Key yields 0.833---a $+0.087$ shift from subset selection alone, more than
six times the field's total claimed progress since 2018---so comparisons against
published numbers are uninterpretable and only same-subset paired tests rank systems.
Under such tests, a small 24-class key CNN that we train on the public Beatport
corpus---pre-registered, with exactly one test evaluation---is statistically
indistinguishable from the madmom CNN ($\Delta{=}-0.001$ \ci{-0.019}{+0.018}) while
carrying no non-commercial license restriction, with the best exact accuracy of any
system measured (0.780). (2)~Feeding ground-truth stems to a
multi-instrument transformer (YourMT3+) yields 0.849 note-F on both bass and dense
polyphonic accompaniment on the Slakh2100 test split, and the full mix$\to$MIDI system
(SCNet~XL $\to$ YourMT3+) reaches 0.788 note-F on accompaniment---\emph{above the
ground-truth-stem ceiling of a widely used lightweight transcriber} (0.490).
(3)~Signal-level separation quality and downstream accuracy can disagree: a
$+2.7$\,dB SI-SDR improvement \emph{reduces} drum-transcription accuracy. (4)~A
pre-registered negative result on EDM structure analysis: three class-rebalanced
training variants all failed their gates, with per-class gains consistently funded by
adjacent-class regressions---evidence that the binding constraint is the training
signal, not model weights or capacity. All code, evaluation scripts, per-track
artifacts, and statistical analyses are public.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

DJ and music-production workflows need reliable, machine-readable answers to a small set
of questions about any track: what key is it in, what tempo, where are the beats and
downbeats, how is it structured (intro, buildup, drop, breakdown), and what are the
constituent parts (stems, and ultimately notes). Electronic dance music is
simultaneously the genre family where these questions are most operationally
important---harmonic mixing, beat-matched transitions, and phrase-aligned mashups are
core practice---and one where general-purpose MIR models underperform: EDM's production
conventions (half-time drum\,\&\,bass, sustained pads with ambiguous modality, drops
that function unlike pop choruses) sit in the tails of the distributions most models are
trained on.

\jams{} is an open-source analysis service built for this use case. It runs locally
(macOS Apple-Silicon and Linux), exposes a single HTTP endpoint, and returns key, tempo,
beat/downbeat grids, functional structure segments, separated stems, and per-stem MIDI
for an arbitrary audio file. Every capability is backed by an evaluation harness in the
same repository: committed acquisition scripts for each public dataset, committed
scoring scripts using \texttt{mir\_eval}~\citep{raffel2014mireval}, an experiments
ledger in which every quantitative claim traces to a script and commit, and a
statistical-analysis script that regenerates every confidence interval in this paper.

We report four contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{A subset-calibration finding} (\S\ref{sec:key}): the strongest published
  GiantSteps~Key system gains $+0.087$ weighted score when re-run on our usable-track
  subset---larger than the total claimed progress across recent published systems---so
  subset-measured numbers are not comparable to published full-set numbers. Only
  same-subset paired tests rank systems; under those, a small
  CNN we train on public data---pre-registered, evaluated once---statistically ties
  the strongest baseline with the best exact accuracy and no non-commercial license
  restriction.
  \item \textbf{Stem-input and end-to-end transcription results}
  (\S\ref{sec:transcription}): we quantify the cascade from mix to stems to MIDI at
  each stage on Slakh2100, including a full-system result that exceeds a popular
  transcriber's ground-truth-stem ceiling.
  \item \textbf{Metric divergence in separator selection} (\S\ref{sec:separation}): stem-level
  SI-SDR and downstream transcription accuracy can disagree in sign---separator
  selection should be scored on the task it feeds.
  \item \textbf{A pre-registered negative result} (\S\ref{sec:structure}): three
  variants of class-rebalanced structure-model training, each gated before results were
  seen, all failed, with every per-class gain funded by an adjacent-class regression.
  The negative result is load-bearing for practitioners tempted by the same obvious
  interventions.
\end{enumerate}

Throughout, we follow one measurement protocol (\S\ref{sec:protocol}): anything learned
is fit on training data only and evaluated once on the test set; all system comparisons
are paired per-track bootstraps on identical track subsets; and structure experiments
were gated by pass/fail criteria committed to the public experiments ledger before
results existed. The same protocol surfaced and replaced a leaked refinement in our own
earlier key detector (\S\ref{sec:key})---the harness audits us on the same terms as the
baselines.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Key detection.} Template (profile-matching) methods date to
Krumhansl~\citep{krumhansl1990cognitive}; the \texttt{edma} profile of Faraldo et
al.~\citep{faraldo2016key,faraldo2017thesis}, shipped in
Essentia~\citep{bogdanov2013essentia}, is tuned for EDM. Convolutional approaches
include the madmom key CNN of Korzeniowski \& Widmer~\citep{korzeniowski2018genre,
bock2016madmom} and later CNN variants~\citep{schreiber2019inception,
steinwedel2021inceptionkeynet}. Self-supervised tonality estimation (STONE, then S-KEY)
learns key structure from unlabeled audio~\citep{kong2024stone,kong2025skey};
KeyMyna~\citep{keymyna2026} applies masked-contrastive pretraining. The GiantSteps~Key
benchmark~\citep{knees2015giantsteps} and its training-purpose companion
GiantSteps-MTG-Keys~\citep{faraldo2017thesis} are the standard EDM key datasets.

\textbf{Transcription.} basic-pitch~\citep{bittner2022basicpitch} is a lightweight,
instrument-agnostic note transcriber in wide production use. Transformer sequence models
(MT3~\citep{gardner2022mt3}; YourMT3+~\citep{chang2024yourmt3}) transcribe
multi-instrument mixtures. Slakh2100~\citep{manilow2019slakh} provides synthesized
multitracks with aligned per-stem MIDI; E-GMD~\citep{callender2020egmd} covers drums;
ADTOF~\citep{zehren2021adtof} provides large-scale non-synthetic drum annotations and an
accompanying model family; MedleyDB~\citep{bittner2014medleydb} provides real-audio
stems and pitch annotations.

\textbf{Source separation.} We evaluate Hybrid Transformer
Demucs~\citep{rouard2023hybrid}, BS-RoFormer~\citep{lu2024bsroformer}, and
SCNet~\citep{tong2024scnet}, using checkpoints from the MSST model
zoo~\citep{zfturbo2024msst}.

\textbf{Structure analysis.} All-In-One~\citep{kim2023allinone} jointly estimates
beats, downbeats, boundaries, and functional labels with neighborhood attention on
demixed audio, trained on the Harmonix Set~\citep{nieto2019harmonix}; Raveform
\citep{kim2026raveform} is the same group's EDM structure corpus, with released
EDM-trained per-fold models that our structure stack runs on-device
(\S\ref{sec:structure}). Dedicated beat trackers form a parallel line, from
multi-task TCNs with DBN decoding~\citep{bock2020deconstruct} to DBN-free
transformers~\citep{foscarin2024beatthis}; \jams{} uses no separate tracker---its
beats and downbeats come from the structure model's beat head, so one ensemble
supplies both the grid and the segments.

\textbf{Evaluation hygiene.} We rely on \texttt{mir\_eval}~\citep{raffel2014mireval}
and \texttt{mirdata}~\citep{bittner2019mirdata} for metrics and dataset handling. Tempo
ground-truth quality for EDM was examined by Schreiber \&
M{\"u}ller~\citep{schreiber2018crowd}, whose GiantSteps~Tempo re-annotation we use for
label corrections (\S\ref{sec:pitfalls}); our subset-calibration finding
(\S\ref{sec:key}) is in the same spirit, quantifying how benchmark-\emph{subset}
selection alone moves scores by more than typical claimed improvements.

\section{The \jams{} system}
\label{sec:system}

\jams{} is a Python service exposing \texttt{POST /v1/analyze}: audio in, JSON out,
with optional stems and MIDI. Two architectural decisions matter for reproducibility:

\textbf{Isolated model workers.} The heavy model stacks (separation, structure,
transcription) have mutually conflicting dependency sets, so each runs in a
self-contained worker subprocess with an inline dependency manifest (PEP~723),
communicating over line-delimited JSON with automatic respawn. Every worker pins its
dependencies; environments are rebuilt deterministically by \texttt{uv}. This is what
makes ``the shipped system'' a well-defined object of evaluation: the exact model
weights, decode thresholds, and library versions that produced the numbers in this paper
are pinned in the repository.

\textbf{No silent fallbacks.} A failure anywhere (missing model, incompatible platform,
worker crash) raises an error; analysis quality never silently varies with environment.
We adopted this contract after observing quality cliffs that would otherwise be
invisible in production (e.g., a 6-stem separator silently dropping guitar/piano content
cost $-0.20$ note-F downstream; \S\ref{sec:separation}).

Table~\ref{tab:system} summarizes the shipped configuration. A companion web application
(waveform + beat grid + segment editor + per-stem piano rolls) serves as the label
inspection and correction tool behind our structure error analyses and as a
demonstration client.

\textbf{Tempo system.} Tempo is a clean-room CNN in the Schreiber--M\"uller
family~\citep{schreiber2018tempocnn} (2.9M parameters, MIT weights): 40-band mel input,
short temporal filters, four multi-filter modules, and a 256-class softmax over
30--285\,BPM with sliding-window averaging, trained on Raveform beat-derived BPM plus
GiantSteps~Tempo~v2 minus the 42 tracks whose Beatport ids appear in GiantSteps~Key.
Like the key CNN it was gated by a pre-registered one-shot evaluation before shipping:
against the pretrained TempoCNN it is statistically indistinguishable---paired
$\Delta$Acc1 (corrected labels) $-0.002$ \ci{-0.015}{+0.011}, $n{=}458$, 449 exact
ties---so the shipped stack replaces third-party tempo weights with our own at no
measured cost. Genre-aware octave resolution is shared by both systems and sits outside
the model.

\textbf{Drum system.} Drums are transcribed by our own convolutional-recurrent network
(1.5M parameters, MIT weights): a 96-band log-mel front end, three convolutional
blocks, a bidirectional GRU, five per-class onset heads (kick/snare/hi-hat/tom/cymbal),
and a velocity regression head, trained on E-GMD plus Slakh oracle and
separation-processed drum stems. It replaces a torch port of the ADTOF weights
(CC~BY-NC-SA~4.0 originals; the port itself declared no license). The pre-registered
one-shot gate found the replacement \emph{superior} on both protocols: Slakh-oracle
macro onset-F $0.767$ vs $0.638$ (paired $\Delta$ $+0.129$ \ci{+0.106}{+0.152},
$n{=}151$) and E-GMD $0.818$ vs $0.645$ ($+0.173$ \ci{+0.155}{+0.191}, $n{=}500$).
The transcription and separation experiment tables later in the paper predate this
swap and report ADTOF in the drum lane where marked.

\textbf{Licensing posture.} The learned components we ship carry no non-commercial
terms: the key, tempo, and drum CNN weights are ours (MIT; 0.45\,MB, 12\,MB, and
6\,MB, trained from public data; the legacy fusion heads likewise ship as MIT JSON),
S-KEY weights are MIT, YourMT3+ weights Apache-2.0, and SCNet checkpoints come from the
MIT-licensed MSST zoo. The one prior exception---the ADTOF drum port---was retired by
the superiority gate above rather than relicensed around. The madmom key CNN
(CC~BY-NC-SA weights) is used for \emph{evaluation only} and never shipped. The
repository itself is MIT-licensed.

\section{Evaluation protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}

\textbf{Datasets.} \emph{Key}: GiantSteps~Key~\citep{knees2015giantsteps} (test only;
$n{=}567$ usable of 604 after dropping atonal/multi-key/unparseable labels---a
documented subset, central to \S\ref{sec:key}); for training, GiantSteps-MTG-Keys
\citep{faraldo2017thesis} (1{,}157 usable high-confidence tracks; used by the earlier
learned refinements) and its revised-annotation superset \texttt{beatport\_key}
(1{,}486 tracks, 1{,}363 usable; used by the key CNN)---the two share one underlying
Beatport corpus, verified to have zero track-id overlap with GiantSteps~Key.
\emph{Transcription/separation}: the Slakh2100 test split~\citep{manilow2019slakh} in
its de-duplicated ``redux'' form ($n{=}151$ multitracks; stems regrouped to
drums/bass/other; ground-truth stems are summed class stems, ground-truth notes merged
class MIDI); a 500-track subset of E-GMD~\citep{callender2020egmd} (isolated electronic
kits); a MedleyDB vocals smoke test ($n{=}1$; access-gated, full benchmark pending).
\emph{Structure}: Raveform~\citep{kim2026raveform}, a 1{,}423-track EDM structure
corpus from the All-In-One authors, with expert beats, downbeats, and functional
segments over an 11-label vocabulary (intro, altintro, buildup, drop, breakdown,
cooldown, bridge, outro, altoutro, plus start/end sentinels following the Harmonix
convention~\citep{nieto2019harmonix}); audio is sourced per track from the annotated
YouTube IDs, of which 1{,}332 remained retrievable for our evaluation. All structure
scoring is held-out per fold: each track is scored by a fold model that did not train
on it. \emph{Tempo}: the GiantSteps~Key tempo labels ($n{=}458$)
with curated corrections from GiantSteps~Tempo~v2~\citep{schreiber2018crowd}
(\S\ref{sec:pitfalls}).

\textbf{Metrics.} Key: MIREX weighted score (1.0 exact, 0.5 fifth, 0.3 relative, 0.2
parallel) and exact accuracy. Tempo: Acc1 (estimate within $\pm4\%$ of the reference
tempo). Transcription:
\texttt{mir\_eval} onset+pitch note-F (50\,ms / 50\,cents, offsets ignored),
instrument-agnostic per stem; drums scored as per-class onset-F (50\,ms) over a 5-class
vocabulary (kick/snare/hi-hats/toms/cymbals), macro-averaged. Separation: SI-SDR versus
ground-truth stems, \emph{plus} transcription-through-separation note-F---the metric
that matters for the product. Structure: beat/downbeat F (70\,ms), boundary hit-rate
HR@0.5s and @3s, pairwise frame clustering F, V-measure; per-class label quality as
GT-duration coverage.

\textbf{Training protocol.} Anything learned is fit on the training corpus
only (for key: GS-MTG or its \texttt{beatport\_key} superset, never GiantSteps~Key),
model-selected by cross-validation \emph{within the training
split}, then evaluated exactly once on the test set. For structure experiments, gates
(pass/fail criteria and their metrics) were pre-registered in the experiments ledger
before results were seen.

\textbf{Statistics.} All CIs are 95\% percentile bootstrap ($10^{4}$ resamples, fixed
seed). System comparisons are \emph{paired}: per-track score differences on the same
tracks, resampled at the track level. We report a difference as significant only when
the paired CI excludes zero. Point comparisons against \emph{published} numbers are
explicitly out of scope as rankings (\S\ref{sec:key} shows why).

\textbf{Systems.} The template row is Essentia's \texttt{edma} profile matcher,
unmodified. \emph{Mode retrain} and \emph{fusion} are the project's earlier learned
refinements---small logistic heads over chroma cues and S-KEY posterior features, fit
on GS-MTG with CV-selected thresholds (exact features and coefficients ship as JSON in
the repository); they are retained in the table as context and as the systems the CNN
superseded. \emph{S-KEY} is the self-supervised tonality estimator of Kong et
al.~\citep{kong2025skey}, run from its released MIT checkpoint. The madmom CNN baseline
was run with its published weights and default
processor settings. The \emph{key CNN} is ours (pre-registered as experiment K10 in
the public ledger): input is a log-magnitude CQT, 24 bins per octave over 8 octaves
from C1 (hop 4096 at 22.05\,kHz); three convolutional blocks (two $3{\times}3$
convolutions with batch normalization and ELU, then $2{\times}2$ max-pooling; 16/32/64
channels) feed a dropout layer, time-only average pooling---frequency structure reaches
the readout, which absolute-pitch tasks require---and a linear 24-way softmax; 109k
parameters, 0.45\,MB, MIT. Training: \texttt{beatport\_key} (1{,}363 usable tracks;
zero test-set overlap, verified by track id), AdamW with a cosine schedule,
${\sim}60$-second random crops, $\pm4$-semitone pitch-shift augmentation via CQT
bin-roll with label transposition; the final model trains on the full corpus at the
CV-selected epoch budget. Inference is a single forward pass over the full-track CQT;
the shipped worker analyzes a two-minute track in under two seconds on Apple-Silicon
CPU, including model load. The experiment was pre-registered with a fixed decision
rule; all selection (architecture, capacity, and standalone-vs-fusion candidates) used
training-corpus CV only, and the locked model received exactly one test evaluation.

\textbf{The calibration finding.} GiantSteps~Key ships 604 annotations, of which 600
have retrievable preview audio in our pull; like prior work, we further drop atonal,
multi-key, and unparseable labels, leaving $n{=}567$ for exact-label scoring. Such subsets are routine and rarely
identical across papers. To measure what that does to scores, we re-ran the strongest
published system---the madmom CNN, published at 0.746 weighted on the full
set~\citep{korzeniowski2018genre}---on our subset: it scores \textbf{0.833}, a
$\mathbf{+0.087}$ shift attributable to subset selection alone
(Figure~\ref{fig:keyforest}). For scale: the total claimed progress across published
systems from 2018 to 2026 (0.746 $\to$ 0.759~\citep{keymyna2026}) is $0.013$---more
than six times smaller. \emph{Any} comparison of a subset-measured number against a published
full-set number is therefore uninterpretable, including favorable comparisons of our own
systems. The only valid rankings are same-subset paired tests.

\textbf{Rankings under paired tests} (Table~\ref{tab:keypaired}): no pairwise
difference among the learned systems reaches significance, and all significantly exceed
the raw template. The sharpest comparison is our key CNN against the madmom CNN:
$\Delta = -0.001$ \ci{-0.019}{+0.018}, with 486 of 567 tracks scored identically---a
statistical dead heat with the strongest published system, from a model trained in two
hours on public data. Under K10's pre-registered rule this is reported as a failure to
establish \emph{superiority}; what it establishes instead is parity without the
non-commercial restriction, plus the best exact accuracy measured (0.780 vs.\ madmom's
0.773). Against the prior fusion system, the CNN gains $+0.020$ \ci{-0.002}{+0.043}
(58 wins / 38 losses) while being strictly simpler---a single small model replaces the
template stage plus two learned heads---so it ships as the production key detector.
All selection was made by training-split cross-validation only (the CNN over wider and
fusion-composed variants by corpus CV), never by test scores.

\textbf{A note on leakage.} An earlier shipped version of the mode refinement had been
fit on GiantSteps~Key itself (0.801 weighted); the strict retrain reported here (0.810)
replaced it before any comparison in this paper was run. The two scores happen to
agree---leakage does not always inflate a number---but only the leakage-free protocol
makes the number defensible.

\textbf{Genre analysis.} Per-genre weighted scores of the shipped CNN range from 0.900
(Dubstep) to 0.739 (Electronica/Downtempo), with Trance (0.763) also weak---sustained,
modally ambiguous textures---and no genre collapses (Figure~\ref{fig:keygenre}).
Notably, Techno, the prior fusion system's weakest genre (0.746), recovers to 0.854
under the CNN.

basic-pitch~\citep{bittner2022basicpitch} by $+0.36$ note-F on polyphonic accompaniment
(``other''), winning on \emph{every one} of 151 tracks, and by $+0.06$ on bass (87\% of
tracks). Both systems were scored with identical scoring functions against identical
ground truth, and basic-pitch's onset/frame thresholds were tuned \emph{in its favor} on
a development subset (worth $+0.023$ note-F on accompaniment over defaults). basic-pitch
is a deliberately lightweight real-time model; the comparison quantifies what a large
transformer buys, not a like-for-like contest. Drums are routed to a dedicated drum
transcriber (ADTOF) in every configuration---velocity measurement and GM-percussion
mapping are drum-specific requirements---so the pitched-transcriber swap leaves the drum
lane unchanged (hence the identical e2e drum scores and the ``---'' cells).

\textbf{End-to-end.} The shipped full system---mix $\to$ SCNet~XL separation $\to$
YourMT3+ (pitched stems) + ADTOF (drums)---reaches 0.788 note-F on accompaniment and
0.661 on bass from the raw mixture, with zero pipeline failures on the test split. Two
observations: (a)~\textbf{the end-to-end system exceeds basic-pitch's oracle ceiling on
accompaniment} (0.788 from the mix vs.\ 0.490 from ground-truth stems)---upgrading the
transcriber matters more than perfecting separation; (b)~separation costs YourMT3+
$-6.1$ points on accompaniment but $-18.7$ points on bass, whose low-frequency content
bleeds most in separation---yet e2e bass still edges the same separator feeding
basic-pitch (0.661 vs.\ 0.645). SI-SDR measured in the e2e run reproduced the
separation A/B's numbers (14.3/11.8/6.0\,dB) on independent hardware, an explicit
cross-run consistency check.

Auxiliary results: ADTOF drums score 0.645 macro onset-F on E-GMD's isolated electronic
kits ($n{=}500$, out-of-domain for the model); a single-track MedleyDB vocals smoke test
gives 0.582 note-F (full benchmark pending dataset access).

\subsection{Structure: a pre-registered negative result}
\label{sec:structure}

The shipped structure stack is the All-In-One~\citep{kim2023allinone} architecture
running the Raveform authors' released EDM-trained per-fold
models~\citep{kim2026raveform} as an on-device ensemble (a state-dict port; no training
of ours). ``Stock'' below always denotes the released member for the evaluated fold, so
both arms of every comparison are strictly held-out on the evaluated tracks; each
experiment's gate (pass/fail criteria and metrics) was committed to the public
experiments ledger before its results existed. On a 104-track held-out CV sample the
stock system reaches beat-F 0.978, downbeat-F 0.964, boundary HR@0.5 0.755, pairwise-F
0.825, consistent with the dataset paper's reported performance; the gate arms below
re-measure it on full held-out folds (fold~2, $n{=}165$: beat-F 0.981, HR@0.5 0.757,
pairwise-F 0.846). Its known weakness is rare-class label quality: buildup and cooldown
segments are frequently absorbed by ``drop'' (coverage ${\approx}0.49$ and $0.48$). This is exactly the failure mode
class-rebalanced training is supposed to fix, so we attempted it---under pre-registered
gates---in three isolated variants:

\begin{description}
  \item[ST-v1: re-weighted from-scratch training.\footnotemark] Inverse-duration
  function-class weights (effective range ${\sim}110{:}1$) with genre-balanced sampling.
  \emph{Failed its gate on two independent folds}: boundary HR@0.5 $-0.15$/$-0.21$,
  pairwise $-0.18$/$-0.16$, and---the tell---beat-F $-0.050$ on \emph{both} folds: the
  shared trunk itself degraded. Rare-class gains on fold~2 (cooldown $+0.25$) reversed
  on fold~1 ($-0.18$) under longer training: progressive damage, not a stable trade.
  A decode-threshold mismatch between the arms was later found and corrected;
  re-scoring moved beat-F by less than $0.01$ and changed no verdict.
  \item[ST-v3: transfer with a frozen trunk.] Stock weights loaded as initialization
  (verified round-trip byte-identical), trunk and beat/section heads frozen, class
  weights capped at $3.0$; only the 1{,}067-parameter function readout trains. Validity
  held by construction and exactly: beats, downbeats, and boundaries byte-identical to
  stock on all 165 held-out tracks. The gate reduced to label quality---and exposed a
  \textbf{class trade} (Figure~\ref{fig:structuretrade}): cooldown $+0.233$
  \ci{+0.157}{+0.312} but buildup $-0.158$ \ci{-0.219}{-0.098} and outro $-0.092$.
  \item[ST-v4: capacity, single variable.] A zero-initialized residual MLP
  (96$\to$64$\to$11; 7{,}990 trainable parameters) added to the same frozen
  configuration, byte-equal to stock at initialization. The capacity \emph{re-routed}
  the trade rather than resolving it: intro/outro/altoutro gains, but buildup
  $-0.240$ \ci{-0.308}{-0.175} (worse than v3), end $-0.355$ \ci{-0.435}{-0.278}, and
  v3's cooldown gain shrank to a non-significant $+0.076$. The apparent bridge gain
  ($+0.125$) rests on $n{=}4$ tracks and is not significant.
\end{description}
\footnotetext{An intermediate head-only variant (ST-v2) was invalidated before
evaluation: checkpoint validation showed the trainer had no pretrained-weight loading,
so its ``frozen'' trunk was randomly initialized. The same check established that ST-v1
was re-weighted from-scratch training rather than fine-tuning; its paired verdicts are
unaffected. ST-v3 is the corrected experiment.}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{figs/fig_structure_trade.pdf}
\caption{Per-class paired coverage deltas versus the stock model (fold-2 held-out,
$n{=}165$ tracks; track-level bootstrap CIs). Both constrained variants keep
beats/boundaries byte-identical to stock, isolating label quality---and both fund

\textbf{Arc conclusion.} Across re-weighted training, a rebalanced frozen readout, and
added readout capacity, \emph{buildup never improved and every gain was funded by an
adjacent-class regression}. With weights, rebalancing, and capacity individually
falsified, the binding constraint is the training signal itself---1{,}332 tracks of
frame-level labels whose least consistently annotated classes (buildup boundaries, end)
are precisely the ones at issue. The stock ensemble ships unchanged. We also stopped
iterating for protocol reasons: fold~2 adjudicated three variants, approaching the limit
of defensible held-out reuse. (A failed gate needs no cross-fold confirmation; a shipped
pass would have required one.) The cheapest future directions are training-free
per-class threshold calibration fit on training folds, and label-definition work.

\subsection{Measurement pitfalls worth publishing}
\label{sec:pitfalls}

Four findings below changed our numbers by more than typical model improvements do;
we report them as first-class results.

\textbf{Bass octave convention.} Slakh bass MIDI follows written convention (sounding
an octave lower). Scoring basic-pitch's sounding-pitch output against it yields 0.04
note-F; applying the $+12$ semitone convention yields 0.80 ($n{=}19$ dev tracks, every
track improved). Any cross-paper bass-transcription comparison that does not state its
octave convention is suspect.

\textbf{Bandwidth artifacts in reduced datasets.} The 16\,kHz ``babyslakh'' preview
dataset caps drum onset-F at ${\sim}0.46$ (vs.\ 0.64 full-bandwidth): hi-hat and cymbal
energy above 8\,kHz simply is not there. The drum transcriber scores 1.0 on its own
44.1\,kHz reference. Dataset previews are for plumbing, not for numbers.

\textbf{Beat-grid quantization.} Snapping transcribed onsets to the analysis beat grid
\emph{costs} accuracy on every stem ($-2.5$/$-0.3$/$-1.7$ points bass/other/drums):
quantization is a stylistic transform and defaults to off.

\textbf{Tempo ground truth.} The GiantSteps~Key tempo labels are wrong for half-time
genres (drum\,\&\,bass labeled ${\sim}87$ where the played tempo is ${\sim}174$). With
raw labels our tempo stack scores 0.919 Acc1; with corrections curated from the
GiantSteps~Tempo~v2 re-annotation~\citep{schreiber2018crowd} plus an explicit
full-tempo convention, 0.967 ($n{=}458$). The correction file is committed and
auditable. Label quality, not model quality, was the larger error source. A related
pitfall from our own history: an early cached baseline artifact predated the genre
metadata that drives octave folding on 13 D\&B/dubstep tracks; every baseline number
here comes from a regeneration with the committed code, archived per track in the
ledger.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\textbf{Subset, not full set.} All GiantSteps~Key numbers are on the $n{=}567$ usable
subset. That is the point of the calibration finding, but it cuts both ways: our
numbers must not be compared against published full-set numbers either. We provide the
subset track list and per-track outputs for future same-subset comparisons.

\textbf{S-KEY checkpoint provenance.} The in-repo S-KEY checkpoint scores 0.817 here
versus 0.721 in its paper~\citep{kong2025skey}; newer weights and subset differences
likely both contribute, and we cannot attribute the gap precisely.

\textbf{Key CNN.} A single training run (no seed variance), selected by
training-corpus CV and evaluated exactly once; parity with madmom is a
failure-to-reject, not demonstrated equivalence, though the CI is tight
(\ci{-0.019}{+0.018}) and 486 of 567 tracks score identically. The model is trained
and evaluated exclusively on EDM; out-of-genre behavior is unmeasured.

\textbf{Synthetic transcription data.} Slakh is sample-rendered; real-mix performance
will differ. Vocals are unmeasured beyond a single smoke test. ADTOF drums are
evaluated out-of-domain on E-GMD (0.645); an in-domain EDM drum benchmark does not yet
exist.

\textbf{Structure.} Single training run per challenger variant (no seed variance);
Raveform audio is YouTube-sourced (annotations were made on the same videos, so there
is no master-mismatch, but 91 of 1{,}423 tracks were no longer retrievable in our
pull); fold~2 adjudicated three variants, so any future \emph{positive} result must be
confirmed on a fresh fold; several rare-class estimates (bridge $n{=}4$) are on thin
support.

\textbf{Baselines.} The madmom key CNN was run with its published weights
(CC~BY-NC-SA), evaluation-only. HT-Demucs-ft and BS-RoFormer separation A/Bs were run
on subsets (50 and 47 tracks) for cost reasons and are marked as such.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

An open, EDM-focused MIR stack assembled from openly licensed components matches the
strongest system we measured to within noise---a pre-registered CNN, trained in two
hours on public data, ties the madmom reference at $\Delta{=}-0.001$---and exceeds
widely used production defaults by wide margins (a mix$\to$MIDI system whose
accompaniment transcription beats a popular transcriber's ground-truth-stem
ceiling). The measurement findings travel beyond this system:
benchmark-subset selection alone can move key-detection scores by more than six times
the field's eight years of claimed progress; SI-SDR can rank separators opposite to the
task they feed; dataset conventions (bass octave, preview bandwidth, half-time tempo
labels) shift scores by amounts that dwarf typical model improvements; and
pre-registered gates with paired statistics make a negative result on class-rebalanced
structure training defensible enough to publish. Hidden-test-set community evaluations
(e.g., the revived MIREX campaigns) are the systemic complement to the same-subset
paired protocol we advocate; we plan to submit the key system for independent
validation.
